**Botanical name:** *Khaya* species (African Mahogany, including Khaya anthotheca — East African Mahogany)

**Origin:** African Mahogany grows across tropical Africa, with the East African species found in Kenya's wetter, high-rainfall regions such as Kakamega Forest, Mount Kenya, the Aberdares, and coastal forests. It remains one of the most prized furniture hardwoods in the region for its balance of beauty, strength, and workability.

### Appearance
Heartwood ranges from pale pink to a deep reddish-brown, often with darker streaks, and develops a richer, deeper tone as it ages. Quartersawn surfaces can show an attractive ribbon-stripe figure, while interlocked grain gives some boards a distinctive chatoyance under light.

### Working Properties
- **Density:** Moderately heavy with medium bending and crushing strength
- **Workability:** Generally easy to work and finishes beautifully, though interlocked grain can cause tearout if not handled carefully
- **Stability:** Good dimensional stability once properly seasoned — performs well in Nairobi's humid climate without excessive movement
- **Durability:** Naturally resistant to termites and fungal decay in the heartwood, though sapwood is more vulnerable

### Best Uses
Mahogany's workability and classic good looks make it a favourite for refined, detailed pieces:
- Dining tables, cabinetry, and sideboards
- Fine joinery and detailed carving work
- Veneer and decorative panelling
- Furniture paired with brass or blackened steel for a warmer, classic-luxury look

### Care & Maintenance
- **Dusting:** Wipe regularly with a soft, dry cloth to prevent buildup and keep the grain looking its best.
- **Sunlight:** Avoid prolonged direct sun exposure — it will fade and unevenly lighten the rich colour over time.
- **Finishing:** Mahogany accepts oil, lacquer, and varnish finishes equally well. A wiping oil/varnish blend suits casual pieces with open pores; a filled-pore lacquer finish suits more formal pieces.
- **Moisture:** Naturally moisture-resistant, which is an advantage in Nairobi's humid spells, but standing liquid should still be wiped promptly to protect the finish.
- **Touch-ups:** Light scuffs can usually be addressed with fine-grit sanding followed by a matching finish coat — ask us about refinishing if a piece has lost its lustre.
